## Design Context

### Users
CMOS is used by HIMATEKKOM ITS organizers and members to run internal operations for the 2026 cabinet. Primary users include admins, BPH, kabinet coordinators, and staff who manage programs, tasks, timelines, evaluations, announcements, internal communication, and shared resources. They use the product as a working system rather than a showcase site, so the interface needs to support focused daily decision-making, quick scanning, and reliable execution on desktop and mobile.

The public-facing pages represent the organization externally and should communicate institutional credibility, current activity, and structured student leadership without feeling stiff or corporate.

### Brand Personality
The brand should feel formal, student-led, and professional. It should communicate organized leadership, accountability, and momentum while still feeling like it belongs to a student organization rather than a generic enterprise tool.

Emotionally, the product should create confidence and clarity first. It can feel ambitious and polished, but not flashy, gamified, or startup-hype driven.

### Aesthetic Direction
Keep the existing purple and gold identity, but execute it with more discipline and consistency. The public site should feel ceremonial and editorial, with stronger composition and a more intentional sense of institution. The authenticated product should feel cleaner and more operational, using the same identity with calmer surfaces and sharper hierarchy.

The default direction is a light-first working application with optional darker accents or sections where they serve emphasis, not decoration. Visual references should be closer to editorial institutional design and premium administrative software than neon dashboards, glassmorphism-heavy startup landing pages, or playful consumer apps.

### Design Principles
1. Prioritize clarity over spectacle. Dense workflows must remain legible, fast to scan, and calm under repeated daily use.
2. Express institutional character without becoming corporate. The interface should feel student-led, organized, and credible.
3. Use the purple-gold identity selectively. Accent colors should guide attention, not flood the interface.
4. Differentiate public and internal contexts. Public pages can be more expressive; operational screens should be more restrained and task-focused.
5. Build reusable Svelte patterns instead of isolated page designs so the whole app feels coherent after the migration.
